NTDS & MALARIA RESEARCH AND COLLABORATION ALLIANCE

NTDs & Malaria Research and Collaboration Alliance in University between TAIWAN and AFRICA


The "Medical Field Alliance" project in 2023 was proposed by Taipei Medical University, targeting universities in two African countries, Swaziland and Nigeria, to develop an alliance in the field of "Neglected Tropical Diseases" (NTDs). The Taiwanese members of the alliance are Taipei Medical University, National Taiwan University, National Defense Medical Center, and National Cheng Kung University. In addition, the alliance includes non-Taiwanese members, namely the University of Swaziland and the Obafemi Awolowo University in Nigeria.

Dr. Wei-Chen Lin ProfessorDepartment of Parasitology, College of Medicine, National Cheng Kung University

Research Interests:
1.Pathogenic Mechanisms and Virulence Factors of Acanthamoeba spp.: Acanthamoeba keratitis is an infectious disease caused by pathogenic Acanthamoeba species that parasitize the cornea. In severe cases, it can lead to corneal ulceration, perforation, and even loss of vision or the entire eyeball. Once Acanthamoeba infects the eye, mucous membranes of wounds, or enters the lungs through the respiratory tract and subsequently reaches the brain via the bloodstream, it can cause granulomatous amoebic encephalitis. In our laboratory, we study the interaction between the parasite and the host to identify potential virulence factors and elucidate the pathogenic mechanisms by which Acanthamoeba damages corneal epithelial cells and the extracellular matrix.
2.Improvement of Drug Therapies for Acanthamoeba and Development of New Treatment Approaches: Polyhexamethylene biguanide (PHMB) is the preferred treatment for Acanthamoeba infections, but its usage is not widespread in Taiwan. Several cases of treatment failure with PHMB have been observed clinically. The mechanism of action of PHMB on the parasite is not thoroughly understood, and studies on the risk assessment of drug resistance, development of drug tolerance or resistance mechanisms by the parasite are lacking. Once drug-resistant strains emerge, the selection and use of subsequent treatment options become challenging. Our laboratory is currently conducting in-depth analysis of the effects of PHMB on Acanthamoeba and simultaneously testing new drugs and treatment approaches to address the challenges of Acanthamoeba infections in Taiwan.

Dr. Shin-Hong Shiao ProfessorDepartment of Tropical Medicine and Parasitology, College of Medicine, National Taiwan University

Research Areas:
1. Vector Cell and Molecular Biology
The research area of vector cell and molecular biology involves studying the interactions between disease vectors (such as mosquitoes, ticks, and fleas) and the pathogens they transmit, with a focus on understanding the cellular and molecular mechanisms underlying vector competence, vector-pathogen interactions, and vector-borne disease transmission. This field encompasses various aspects, including the biology and physiology of vector cells, vector immune responses, vector microbiota, vector genetics, and the molecular basis of vector-borne disease transmission.
Researchers in this field investigate the molecular and cellular processes that occur within vector cells, such as the midgut, salivary glands, and reproductive tissues, to elucidate how pathogens establish infection, replicate, and interact with the vector's immune system. They also study the genetic factors that contribute to vector competence and vector resistance to pathogens. Additionally, researchers explore the role of vector-associated microbiota in modulating vector competence and transmission of diseases.
Through the study of vector cell and molecular biology, researchers aim to enhance our understanding of the complex interactions between vectors and pathogens, identify novel targets for vector control strategies, develop innovative approaches to interrupt disease transmission cycles, and ultimately mitigate the impact of vector-borne diseases on human and animal health.

2. Parasite Molecular Biology
Parasite molecular biology is a research field that focuses on studying the genetic and molecular aspects of parasites. It involves the investigation of the genetic makeup, gene expression, replication, metabolism, and interactions of parasites with their hosts and environments at the molecular level. This field encompasses a wide range of parasites, including protozoa, helminths, and arthropods.
Researchers in this field utilize molecular techniques and tools to understand the fundamental biology of parasites. They study the genomes, transcriptomes, and proteomes of parasites to identify important genes and proteins involved in various biological processes, such as host recognition and invasion, immune evasion, reproduction, drug resistance, and pathogenesis. By elucidating the molecular mechanisms underlying these processes, researchers aim to uncover potential targets for the development of new therapeutics, diagnostics, and vaccines against parasitic diseases.
Parasite molecular biology also involves investigating the genetic diversity and population dynamics of parasites, including their evolution and transmission patterns. Researchers use molecular markers and genetic analysis techniques to understand the epidemiology, transmission routes, and drug resistance profiles of parasites. This knowledge can help guide the development of effective control and prevention strategies for parasitic infections.
Overall, the field of parasite molecular biology plays a crucial role in advancing our understanding of parasite biology, host-parasite interactions, and the development of strategies to combat parasitic diseases.

3. Virology
Virology is the scientific field dedicated to the study of viruses, which are microscopic infectious agents that can infect a wide range of organisms, including humans, animals, plants, and even bacteria. Virologists investigate various aspects of viruses, including their structure, classification, replication cycle, pathogenesis, and interactions with the host immune system.
Research in virology encompasses a diverse range of topics. Virologists study viral morphology, composition, and genetic material to understand the structural and functional characteristics of different viruses. They investigate the mechanisms by which viruses enter host cells, replicate their genetic material, assemble new viral particles, and exit the infected cells to spread to other cells or individuals.
Virology also involves studying the host-virus interactions and the immune response to viral infections. Virologists investigate how viruses evade the host immune system and manipulate cellular processes to establish infection. They explore the molecular mechanisms underlying viral pathogenesis, including the development of disease symptoms, tissue tropism, and the progression of viral infections.
In addition, virologists play a crucial role in the development of antiviral therapies and vaccines. They explore potential targets for antiviral drugs, investigate the mechanisms of drug resistance, and contribute to the design and testing of vaccines to prevent viral infections.
The field of virology has significant implications for public health, as it provides insights into the prevention, control, and treatment of viral diseases. Virologists work to understand the biology of viruses and apply their knowledge to develop strategies for the detection, diagnosis, and management of viral infections, ultimately aiming to mitigate the impact of viral diseases on human and animal health.
4. Medical Entomology
Medical entomology is a specialized field of study that focuses on insects and arthropods of medical importance, particularly those that have an impact on human health. This interdisciplinary field combines knowledge from entomology (the study of insects) and medicine to understand the biology, behavior, and control of medically significant arthropods and the diseases they transmit.
Medical entomologists investigate a wide range of arthropods, including mosquitoes, ticks, fleas, lice, and mites, that are vectors or carriers of various pathogens. They study the life cycles, feeding habits, breeding habitats, and distribution patterns of these arthropods to better understand their role in disease transmission. By identifying and studying the specific vectors responsible for transmitting diseases, medical entomologists can develop effective strategies for vector control and disease prevention.
The field of medical entomology also involves the study of arthropod-borne diseases, such as malaria, dengue fever, Lyme disease, and Zika virus infection. Researchers investigate the interactions between pathogens and their vector arthropods, the mechanisms by which pathogens are transmitted, and the epidemiology of vector-borne diseases. They also explore ways to interrupt the transmission cycle, such as developing insecticides, insect repellents, and vector control measures.
Medical entomology plays a critical role in public health by providing knowledge and strategies to prevent and control vector-borne diseases. Through their research, medical entomologists contribute to the development of effective surveillance systems, diagnostic methods, and targeted interventions to reduce the burden of arthropod-borne diseases on human populations.

Research Areas:
Parasite Drug Resistance Mechanisms
Parasite drug resistance refers to the enhanced ability of parasites to withstand the effects of drug treatments, rendering traditional antiparasitic drugs ineffective in effectively suppressing or eliminating the parasites. Understanding the mechanisms underlying parasite drug resistance is crucial for developing strategies to combat this problem.
Parasites employ various mechanisms to develop resistance to drugs. Some common mechanisms include:
1. Altered drug target: Parasites can undergo genetic changes that alter the target molecules of the drugs, reducing their binding affinity and rendering the drugs less effective.

5. DNA repair mechanisms: Some parasites can enhance their DNA repair systems, enabling them to quickly repair DNA damage induced by antiparasitic drugs, thereby minimizing the drugs' impact on parasite survival.
Understanding these mechanisms is essential for the development of new antiparasitic drugs and strategies to overcome or prevent drug resistance. Researchers in the field of parasitology are actively studying these mechanisms to identify novel targets for drug development and to improve treatment outcomes in parasitic infections.

Signaling Pathways in Parasite-Induced Inflammation:
Parasite infections can trigger inflammatory responses in the host, leading to the recruitment of immune cells, release of inflammatory mediators, and tissue damage. The signaling pathways involved in parasite-induced inflammation play a critical role in coordinating and regulating the host immune response. Here are some key signaling pathways implicated in parasite-induced inflammation:
1. Toll-like receptor (TLR) pathway: TLRs recognize specific pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs) expressed by parasites. Upon binding to PAMPs, TLRs activate intracellular signaling cascades, including the MyD88-dependent pathway, leading to the production of pro-inflammatory cytokines such as interleukin-1 (IL-1), tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-α), and interleukin-6 (IL-6).
2. Nod-like receptor (NLR) pathway: NLRs are cytosolic receptors that detect intracellular PAMPs or danger-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs) released during parasite infection. Activation of NLRs triggers the assembly of inflammasomes, multiprotein complexes that activate caspase-1 and promote the maturation and release of pro-inflammatory cytokines, including IL-1β and IL-18.
3. Cytokine signaling pathways: Pro-inflammatory cytokines, such as TNF-α, IL-1, and IL-6, play a central role in parasite-induced inflammation. These cytokines bind to their respective receptors on target cells, activating downstream signaling pathways such as the NF-κB pathway and the mitogen-activated protein kinase (MAPK) pathway, leading to the production of additional pro-inflammatory cytokines, chemokines, and inflammatory mediators.
4. Interferon (IFN) signaling pathway: Parasite infections can induce the production of type I and type II IFNs, which bind to their receptors on host cells and activate the Janus kinase/signal transducer and activator of transcription (JAK/STAT) pathway. Activation of this pathway results in the upregulation of genes involved in immune responses, including the production of antimicrobial peptides and the activation of immune effector cells.
5. Inflammasome pathway: As mentioned earlier, activation of inflammasomes leads to the release of mature IL-1β and IL-18, which further amplify the inflammatory response. Inflammasome activation occurs through different pathways, including the NLRP3 inflammasome pathway, which is implicated in various parasitic infections.
These are just a few examples of the signaling pathways involved in parasite-induced inflammation. The activation and regulation of these pathways are highly complex and can vary depending on the specific parasite and host interactions. Understanding these signaling pathways is essential for developing targeted therapeutic approaches to modulate the immune response and mitigate the damaging effects of parasite-induced inflammation.

Oluyomi Sowemimo ProfessorDepartment of Zoology, Obafemi Awolowo University

Professor Oluyomi Sowemimo completed his Bachelor's degree in Science at Obafemi Awolowo University in Ile-Ife, Nigeria, and obtained his Master's and Ph.D. degrees in Zoology in 1990. In 1997, he joined Obafemi Awolowo University as a Research Assistant and actively participated in parasitology research at the institution in 2000 and 2007. In 2009, he was promoted to the position of Senior Lecturer.
Currently, he serves as a Visiting Associate Professor at the Nigerian Institute of Medical Research (NIMR) and Taipei Medical University. Professor Sowemimo is involved in numerous local and international organizations and societies, including the British Society for Parasitology and the Nigerian Society for Parasitology and Public Health.
Professor Sowemimo is a parasitologist with a keen interest in medical parasitology and public health. His research primarily focuses on parasitic epidemiology and public health. He has received several international awards, including the Bill and Melinda Gates Foundation Global Health Travel Award. He has attended and presented at international conferences for four consecutive years (2006, 2008-2009, 2014) and has published 34 research papers in international journals.
